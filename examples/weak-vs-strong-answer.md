# Weak vs. Strong Answer Examples

These are illustrative before/after pairs drawn from the patterns in the YC Roaster evaluation rubrics. All company details are fictional. The goal is to show the specific changes that move an answer from weak to strong.

---

## Company Description (50 characters)

**Weak:** "AI-powered platform for enterprise digital transformation" *(56 chars, over limit, meaningless)*

**Strong:** "Automates SOC2 compliance audits for startups" *(46 chars, specific buyer, specific action)*

**What changed:** Removed adjectives, named the specific buyer, described the literal thing the product does.

---

## Product Description

**Weak:**
> We are building a platform that leverages AI to enable companies to streamline their compliance workflows and reduce operational overhead, ultimately allowing them to focus on their core business.

**Strong:**
> We built a SaaS tool that automates the evidence collection for SOC2 audits. When a company connects their AWS, GitHub, and Okta accounts, we continuously collect the screenshots, logs, and access records auditors require. Audits that took 3 months of manual work now take 2 weeks. We launched in January and have 4 paying customers at $500/month.

**What changed:** Removed "platform" and "leverage" and "streamline." Named what the product literally does. Named the integrations. Named the outcome with specifics. Added current state with numbers.

---

## Traction

**Weak:**
> We're seeing strong early traction with thousands of users interested in our product. Our waitlist has grown significantly and we have partnerships in progress with several major players in the industry.

**Strong:**
> We launched in February. We have 87 active users, defined as users who ran at least one analysis in the last 7 days. 22 of those are paying $49/month. MoM growth is 34% over the last 3 months. Churn is 8% monthly — we know this is high and have identified the root cause as onboarding friction; we shipped a new onboarding flow last week. We have no partnerships signed — two conversations are in progress but we haven't counted those as traction.

**What changed:** Named the specific metric and its definition. Gave paying customer count and price. Gave growth rate with time period. Named churn and acknowledged the problem. Dropped the partnership claim that wasn't real yet.

## Why This Idea (Founder-Market Fit)

**Weak:**
> We identified a gap in the market for compliance automation after researching the space extensively. We believe companies are underserved in this area and that our team's background in enterprise software makes us well-positioned to address this opportunity.

**Strong:**
> I spent 3 years as a security engineer at a Series B startup. We went through a SOC2 audit and I personally spent 4 months collecting evidence — pulling screenshots, writing policy docs, chasing down engineering leads for records. We spent $80K on an auditor and still failed the first time. I quit to build the tool I wished existed. My co-founder ran the compliance program at [company], so we've both lived both sides of the audit. We've talked to 35 security engineers at companies that have done SOC2 in the last year. 31 of them described the same 4-month evidence collection nightmare.

**What changed:** Replaced "identified a gap" with a specific lived experience. Named what the experience cost in time and money. Named the co-founder's relevant background. Added the customer discovery with a number.

---

## Competitors

**Weak:**
> Our main competitors are Vanta and Drata. We differentiate through our better user interface, faster onboarding, and more competitive pricing.

**Strong:**
> Vanta and Drata serve Series A+ companies with dedicated security teams who can navigate a complex setup process — they charge $15K–$40K/year and require weeks of implementation. Our observation from talking to potential customers is that pre-Series A companies (fewer than 50 employees, no dedicated security team) are systematically underserved: they need SOC2 to close enterprise deals but can't afford the implementation overhead. We charge $500/month with a self-serve setup that takes 2 hours. Vanta can't easily serve this segment because their enterprise sales motion requires high ACV to justify CAC.

**What changed:** Named competitors but then immediately stated a structural insight, not a feature list. Named the specific buyer segment competitors are ignoring and why. Explained the structural reason competitors can't just copy the approach. Tied the pricing to the motion.

---

## Go-To-Market

**Weak:**
> We plan to grow through SEO, product-led growth, and eventually a sales team as we scale. We will also leverage partnerships with accounting firms and law firms who work with startups.

**Strong:**
> Our first 8 customers came from posting in the YC Slack's compliance channel. The conversion from post to signup was 15%; signup to paid was 40% within 2 weeks. We've now posted in 6 startup Slack communities and are seeing consistent 10–15% signup rates. Our motion is: post in a community → free trial → self-serve conversion within 14 days. At $500/month, the CAC payback is under 2 months. We haven't tried sales yet; we're learning the self-serve motion first. Partnerships aren't part of our current plan — we'd consider it after we've proven the self-serve motion works.

**What changed:** Replaced channel listing with a description of what's actually working. Named the specific channel, the conversion rate at each step, and what the payback looks like. Dropped the partnership claim that wasn't real. Was honest about what they haven't tried yet.

## Team

**Weak:**
> Our team has 20+ years of combined experience in enterprise software and security. Our CEO has an MBA from Wharton and previously worked at McKinsey. Our CTO has a computer science degree from MIT and has built software at Fortune 500 companies.

**Strong:**
> I (CEO) ran the compliance function at [company] for 2 years — I was the person submitting evidence to auditors, the person who failed audits, and the person who rebuilt the program from scratch. My co-founder (CTO) built the internal compliance tooling at [company] that handled 4 SOC2 audits. She's written production code for 8 years. We've been working together for 6 months, both full-time since January. Our current product is entirely her code — she shipped the MVP in 6 weeks.

**What changed:** Replaced credentials with specific experiences relevant to the problem. Named what the CTO actually built and how fast. Showed working history. Confirmed full-time commitment with a specific date.
