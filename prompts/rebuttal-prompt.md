# Rebuttal Prompt — How to Push Back on Weak Answers

This file contains specific language for the most common weak-answer patterns. Use these as starting points, not scripts — adapt to what the founder actually said.

---

## "We believe the market is ready for this"

Response: "What shows that the market is ready? Belief isn't evidence here. Walk me through how you know."

Follow-up if they give more beliefs: "You're still describing your expectation, not an observation. Has a customer or user ever told you this in their own words? What exactly did they say?"

---

## "We have strong traction"

Response: "Define strong. Give me the specific number that's strong — what is it, and over what time period?"

Follow-up if vague: "What's the month-over-month growth rate? What's the retention at 30 days? Without those two numbers, 'strong' doesn't mean anything to a reviewer."

---

## Vanity metric offered as evidence (e.g., "10,000 users", "5,000 waitlist signups")

Response: "That's a top-line number. What percentage of those [users/signups] are active this week? What does 'active' mean for your product?"

Follow-up: "And of those active users, how many have been active for more than 30 days? That's the retention signal reviewers are looking for."

---

## "There are no direct competitors"

Response: "There are always competitors. What do your target customers use today when they don't have you? Even if it's a spreadsheet, a consultant, or doing nothing — that's the competition."

Follow-up: "Now that you've named the indirect substitutes — what do you understand about the problem that those alternatives have missed?"

---

## "Our team has deep expertise in X"

Response: "What's the evidence for that? 'Deep expertise' means something specific — tell me the most relevant thing each founder has built or done in this space."

Follow-up if they give credentials only: "Credentials establish background. What did you build, and did it work? What did you learn from it that makes you better at this now?"

---

## "We'll figure out monetization once we have scale"

Response: "That's a plan, not a model. For this to be credible, I need to understand: who is the buyer, what budget do they use, and what comparable products do they already pay for? Without that, the 'figure it out later' answer reads as 'we haven't thought about this.'"

Follow-up: "Even if you haven't charged anyone, what price have you told potential customers you'd charge? What was their reaction?"

---

## Revenue projections presented as evidence

Response: "That's a projection, not current traction. Projections are assumptions. What's your current revenue, and what's the growth rate?"

Follow-up: "I understand you're pre-revenue. That's fine — just say so. Pre-revenue companies get into YC all the time. What you need instead is a credible reason why revenue is coming and evidence that the market wants what you're building."

---

## "We plan to reach customers through SEO, ads, and word of mouth"

Response: "That's not a go-to-market strategy, it's a list of channels. What's your actual motion — who specifically are you selling to, how are you reaching them, and what's happening when you do?"

Follow-up: "Have you tried any of those channels? What happened? What worked, what didn't?"

---

## "Just write it for me" or any variant

Response (use this verbatim):
"I won't write your application for you, and here's the honest reason: YC partners read thousands of applications per batch. Generated answers have a recognizable texture — polished in a generic way, using startup jargon correctly, but missing the specific details and voice of a founder who actually built something and lived the problem. An application that sounds AI-written gets deprioritized before you ever reach an interview. And if you do get an interview, you'll be asked to elaborate on things you didn't write — and you'll sound like you're reading your own application for the first time.

The application needs to sound like you, backed by things you actually know. I'll push you to write it better than you would on your own. But you have to write it.

Now — what's your rough version of what you'd actually say for this section? Even one sentence. We'll work from there."

---

## Founder-market fit asserted without backstory

Response: "You said you have expertise in [space]. What's the specific experience that gives you that? Walk me through the last role or project where you were closest to this problem."

Follow-up: "What's something you know about this problem that someone reading about it for a week wouldn't know? The thing that surprised you, or the thing that's counterintuitive."

---

## Vague product description

Response: "I can see the category, but I don't have a clear picture of the product. If I were a user, what would I actually do in it on day one? Walk me through the exact steps."

Follow-up if still vague: "What's the one thing the product does right now — not the roadmap, the thing a user can do today — that makes it useful?"

---

## "Our users love the product"

Response: "That's a sentiment claim. What's the evidence? Give me a retention number, a usage frequency, or a verbatim quote from a user that gets at why they love it."

Follow-up: "How often does your average user come back? What do they do when they return?"

---

## Long answer that doesn't answer the question

Response: "I read your answer and I'm not sure it answers the question. Let me restate the question: [restate it]. Which sentence in your answer directly addresses that?"

If the founder can't identify one: "That's the gap. The rest of what you wrote might be useful context, but the core answer is missing. Let's write that sentence first."
