# Successful Patterns — What Strong YC Applications Look Like

Source: distilled from YC Roaster's production evaluation rubrics (`src/lib/ai-judge.ts`). These are the signals that score well across all 7 dimensions.

---

## Cross-cutting principles

The single strongest pattern in successful applications is **evidence over assertion**. Every claim is backed by a number, a named customer, a specific behavior, or a verifiable fact. The weakest applications make claims of equal or greater confidence with none of these.

**Specific beats vague, always.** "We have 10 paying customers at $500/month with 90% retention" beats "we're seeing strong traction." "We talked to 40 potential customers over 6 weeks; 8 asked us to build it and 3 agreed to be design partners" beats "we've validated demand."

## Dimension-by-dimension patterns

### Application Completeness

- Clear explanation of what you're building and who it's for, in plain English, in the first two sentences
- The 50-character description and the full product description are consistent and reinforce each other
- Concrete progress: what exists today, what's been shipped, what's next with timelines
- Clear founder details: who does what, how long they've been working on this, full-time commitment
- Equity and legal structure addressed without ambiguity
- Minimal "Not provided" or blank answers — every unanswered question is a missed signal

### Early Traction

**For post-launch companies:**
- Durable usage metrics with denominators: "47% of users who activated in week 1 returned in week 4"
- Month-over-month growth rates: "22% MoM revenue growth for the last 4 months"
- Revenue quality: recurring subscriptions, expanding accounts, low churn
- Specific paying customers: "3 enterprise customers at $2K/month, signed in the last 6 weeks"
- Organic pull signals: word-of-mouth, inbound that converts, referrals

**For pre-launch companies:**
- Named user discovery: "talked to 40 potential customers at [specific companies], learned X"
- Credible demand commitments: signed LOIs, deposits, design partner agreements
- Prototype testing with behavioral signals: "8 out of 10 users completed the core task in under 2 minutes"
- Velocity signals: how fast they're building and iterating

### Team Quality

- Proof of shipping: specific past products with user or revenue numbers ("built X which got to 5K users / $30K ARR before we shut it down")
- Founder-market fit demonstrated, not asserted: "I spent 3 years as a [role] at [company] dealing with this exact problem"
- Technical coverage: someone on the team can build what they're describing without hiring
- Complementary skills: the business/domain person and the builder are clearly differentiated
- Full-time commitment: founders who quit their jobs to build this signal conviction
- Working history together: co-founders who have built things together before are lower risk

### Market

- Clear buyer with acute pain: not "enterprises" but "mid-market logistics companies with 50–200 drivers who are losing $X/month to route inefficiency"
- Believable wedge and expansion path: starting narrow, growing logically
- Why-now argument grounded in a real change: regulation, technology shift, behavior change, infrastructure availability
- Willingness-to-pay evidence beyond surveys: people who have paid or committed to pay
- Niche that is a wedge into something large — small + defensible beats large + crowded

### Go-To-Market

- Named ICP with specifics: job title, company size, budget ownership, buying trigger
- Distribution wedge that's non-obvious: "we get in through [specific channel/community/integration] that competitors can't use"
- Working motion with metrics: "outbound to [persona] via [channel], 15% reply rate, 30% close rate on qualified calls"
- Pricing aligned to the motion: self-serve pricing makes sense at low ACV; sales motion makes sense at high ACV
- Evidence of learning: "we tried X, it didn't work, now we're doing Y because Z"

### Scalability

- Low marginal cost as a structural property, not an aspiration
- If starting manual: a credible, specific automation roadmap tied to what they're learning manually
- Structural advantages described mechanically: "the more data we collect, the better our model gets, which means our accuracy improves and competitors can't catch up without the data"
- Architecture that was designed to scale, described with specifics
- Not a consultancy: the value delivered doesn't scale linearly with founder time

### Monetization

- Simple, credible pricing tied to value: "we charge $X per [unit] because [buyer] budgets in those units"
- Proof of willingness to pay: revenue, LOIs with dollar amounts, discovery conversations where price wasn't an objection
- Unit economics direction even if early: cost to serve, gross margin, payback period
- Clear purchase motion: who signs off, how long the cycle takes, what triggers a purchase
- Bottom-up TAM: "there are 50K [ICPs] in the US, we can reach 10% of them, at $5K ACV that's a $25M opportunity in the initial market"

## Voice and tone patterns that score well

- Active voice: "we built X" not "X was built"
- Past tense for what's done, present tense for what's running, future tense sparingly and tied to specifics
- Numbers with context: not "10X growth" but "10X growth from $2K to $20K MRR over 4 months"
- Named customers without necessarily naming them publicly: "a top-5 US bank" or "a $2B logistics company" is fine
- Confidence without hedging: state what you know, acknowledge what you don't know rather than softening everything
