# Team — "Who writes code, or does other technical work on your product?"

Also covers: "Was any of it done by a non-founder?" and "Are you looking for a cofounder?"

---

## What YC is actually evaluating

YC is checking whether the founding team can build the product. A team where no founder writes code is a structural red flag — it means technical decisions depend on people who don't have equity and can walk away. Beyond technical coverage, YC is evaluating founder-market fit (does the team have an unfair advantage from lived experience?), commitment (are they all-in?), and complementarity (do the founders cover the key dimensions of the business without gaps?).

Solo founders are not disqualifying, but they carry extra scrutiny: YC wants to see execution velocity that compensates for the single point of failure.

---

## What strong answers contain

- A named person who writes production code, with a description of what they've specifically built in this product
- Evidence of prior shipping: a past product, system, or company with user or revenue numbers attached
- Clear role division: who builds, who sells, who knows the domain
- Full-time commitment from everyone, or a specific and near-term plan to get there
- Co-founder working history: built something together before, know each other well
- For solo founders: a track record of rapid, independent execution that compensates for the team risk

---

## What weak answers look like

- "We're looking for a CTO" — no technical capability on the team right now
- "Our technical co-founder has 15 years of experience" — credentials without evidence of shipping
- "We use contractors to build the product" — technical work is entirely outside the equity structure
- "We all contribute to the product" — roles are unclear, no one is clearly accountable for building
- "We've been working on this nights and weekends" — part-time with no commitment plan
- "I handle the business side and my co-founder handles the technical side" — fine, but needs proof of what "technical side" means in practice

---

## Probing questions to ask the founder

1. "Who in the founding team wrote the last 10 commits? What did they do?"
2. "What's the last thing [technical co-founder] shipped before this company? Did it work?"
3. "Is everyone on the team full-time? If not, when does that change, and what's the trigger?"
4. "Have you worked together before? What did you build, and how did it go?"
5. "If you're a solo founder: what did you build last month? Walk me through it."

---

## Evidence to request from the founder's local files

- **Git log**: `git log --oneline --format='%an %s' | head -30` — shows who is actually committing and what
- **GitHub contributor graph** — if accessible, shows velocity and distribution of work
- **README or codebase structure** — to verify the technical founder actually understands what's been built
- **Past product links or demos** — from before this company, to prove shipping history
- **LinkedIn** is acceptable context for background but push for shipped-product evidence, not credentials

---

## Coaching note on cofounder search

If the founder is actively looking for a co-founder, they should be direct about it and explain what gap they're filling. Hiding this looks worse than admitting it. The answer "yes, we're looking for a technical co-founder — here's why we can still make progress without one" is more credible than pretending the team is complete.
