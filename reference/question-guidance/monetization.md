# Monetization — "How do or will you make money? How much could you make?"

---

## What YC is actually evaluating

YC is not expecting certainty on monetization — they fund many pre-revenue companies. What they are checking is whether the founders have thought clearly about value capture: who pays, why they pay, what they pay, and whether the model can reach a meaningful scale. The "how much could you make" part is asking for a bottom-up estimate, not a projection from a top-down market size.

---

## What strong answers contain

- A named pricing model with a rationale: "SaaS at $200/month per seat because our buyers budget in seat-based tools and that's what they compare us to"
- Evidence of willingness to pay: paying customers, LOIs with dollar amounts, discovery calls where price wasn't an objection
- Unit economics direction even if rough: "cost to serve a customer is about $20/month, we price at $200 — leaves room for CAC and growth"
- Clear buyer and purchase motion: who approves the purchase, who pays, what triggers a buying decision
- Bottom-up TAM reasoning: "there are 80K mid-market manufacturers in the US, we can reach 10% in 3 years, at $12K ACV that's ~$100M"
- For pre-revenue: a credible pricing hypothesis and why it's grounded in buyer behavior

---

## What weak answers look like

- "We will monetize through advertising eventually" — almost never works for B2B; requires massive scale for B2C
- "We'll charge $X per [unit] once we have users" — no evidence of WTP
- Revenue projections without underlying assumptions: "we'll reach $10M ARR in 3 years"
- Pricing copied directly from competitors without evidence it works for the specific segment
- "We'll figure out monetization after we have scale" — acceptable for some consumer plays but must be justified
- Commission-based models with no current transaction volume to anchor the estimate

---

## Probing questions to ask the founder

1. "Have you charged anyone yet? What happened when you first asked for money?"
2. "What's your current pricing? Why that number — not half of it, not double?"
3. "Who approves the purchase decision at your target customer? What does that process look like?"
4. "What's your gross margin at current pricing? What does it converge to at scale?"
5. "Build me a bottom-up market estimate: how many buyers, at what price, reachable by when?"

---

## Evidence to request from the founder's local files

- Revenue records or payment history (even one payment is meaningful)
- Any pricing page, pricing experiments, or pricing conversation notes
- Customer discovery notes where willingness to pay came up
- Comparable pricing data from the competitive landscape they've researched

---

## Coaching note on the TAM question

The "how much could you make" question is an invitation to show bottom-up thinking. Top-down TAM answers ("the market is $19B") tell a reviewer nothing about whether this specific product at this specific price can reach a meaningful number. The bottom-up version ("there are 80K of my target buyers, I can reach 10% at my current ACV in 3 years") is what YC actually wants. Push founders to build this from the specific buyer up, not from the market report down.
