# Company Description — "Describe what your company does in 50 characters or less"

---

## What YC is actually evaluating

This is the hardest question in the application and the first thing a YC partner reads. The 50-character constraint is deliberate: it forces brutal clarity about the core value proposition. If you can't say what you do in 50 characters, you either don't have a clear enough product or you don't understand your own business well enough yet. A strong one-liner instantly communicates the subject (who it's for), the verb (what you do), and the object (what outcome). Adjectives are banned by the constraint.

---

## What strong answers contain

- A subject, a verb, and an object: "[Product] [does X] for [buyer]"
- The specific customer or user — not "enterprises" or "users" but the actual person
- The actual action the product takes — not "transforms" or "powers" but what it literally does
- No adjectives: no "AI-powered", no "next-generation", no "seamless"
- Under 50 characters — if it's over, it's wrong on the form
- Passes the test: a smart person who knows nothing about your space understands it in one read

Strong examples (anonymized patterns):
- "Bookkeeping software for Shopify sellers" (41 chars)
- "API to add video calls to any app" (33 chars)
- "Automates SOC2 compliance audits" (32 chars)
- "Background checks in 24 hours" (29 chars)

---

## What weak answers look like

- "AI-powered platform for enterprise digital transformation" — adjectives, no specificity, over 50 chars
- "We build the operating system for X" — the word "build" is the founder talking, not the product
- "Revolutionizing logistics through data" — what does that mean?
- "The Stripe for healthcare" — analogy without content; fine as a supplement, not as the description
- Anything starting with "We" — the description is about the product, not the founders
- Anything that could describe 10 different companies

---

## Probing questions to ask the founder

1. "Read that out loud to someone who doesn't know your space. Does it say what you DO or what you ARE?"
2. "Remove every adjective from your description. What's left? Is it still accurate?"
3. "Who is the specific person or company this serves? Is that clear from the 50 characters?"
4. "Is the verb in your description what the product literally does — or is it what you aspire to enable?"
5. "Count the characters. Is it actually under 50?"

---

## Evidence to request from the founder's local files

- README first line or project tagline — often the most honest early version of the one-liner
- Any existing marketing copy (landing page headline, product hunt tagline)
- How users describe the product when they refer others ("I use [X], it's a [description]")

---

## Coaching note

The 50-character one-liner and the full product description (next question) must be consistent and reinforce each other. If the one-liner says "bookkeeping for Shopify sellers" but the product description describes a general finance platform, there's a contradiction that suggests unclear positioning. Fix both.
