# Idea Origin — "Why did you pick this idea to work on?"

Full question: "Do you have domain expertise in this area? How do you know people need what you're making?"

---

## What YC is actually evaluating

This is the founder-market fit question. YC is asking: why are you specifically the right person to build this? The answer needs to show one of three things — lived experience with the problem, deep domain expertise from a relevant career, or unusually close proximity to the customer (talked to many, worked with them, are one of them). A team that "saw a market opportunity" is much weaker than a team that has been frustrated by this problem for years and finally decided to fix it themselves.

The second half — "how do you know people need this" — is the demand validation question. YC wants evidence, not conviction.

---

## What strong answers contain

- A specific origin story: "I spent 3 years doing X and was frustrated by Y every single time"
- Domain expertise grounded in work history: "I was a [role] at [company] and dealt with this problem directly for [N] years"
- Specific demand validation: "I talked to 30 potential customers; 8 said they'd switch to this immediately and 3 are already design partners"
- Insider knowledge of the problem space that someone reading about it for a week wouldn't have
- A clear reason why this team has an advantage that others don't: access, expertise, relationships, insight
- Willingness to name specific people who told them they need this

---

## What weak answers look like

- "We saw a market opportunity in X" — no personal connection, no insider knowledge
- "The market is large and growing" — market analysis, not demand proof
- "We ran a survey and 80% said they'd use this" — surveys are not demand proof; people lie on surveys
- "We talked to potential customers" without naming how many, what they said, or whether they committed to anything
- Founder-market fit asserted without backstory: "We have deep expertise in logistics" with no evidence
- "Users told us they want this" without specifics or names

---

## Probing questions to ask the founder

1. "When did you first encounter this problem personally? Walk me through what happened."
2. "What do you know about this space that someone reading about it for a week wouldn't know?"
3. "Name two potential customers you've talked to. What did they tell you, exactly? Did any of them ask you to build this before you started?"
4. "Why you specifically? What's the unfair advantage you have over 5 other teams who could theoretically build this?"
5. "What's the one insight about this problem that your competitors are missing?"

---

## Evidence to request from the founder's local files

- Customer discovery notes or interview transcripts — even rough bullet points from calls
- Any written communications from potential customers asking for the product
- Past work history relevant to the problem (LinkedIn is context; shipped work is evidence)
- Competitive research they've done that reveals the insight competitors are missing

---

## Coaching note on the demand validation trap

"We know people need this because we talked to them" is much stronger than "we know people need this because the market is large." But even talking to people has a floor: a handful of conversations with friends or former colleagues is less credible than 30+ structured conversations with target buyers who have no relationship obligation to be nice. Push for numbers, push for whether any of them committed to anything.
