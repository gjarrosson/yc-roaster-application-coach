# Competitors — "Who are your competitors? What do you understand about your business that they don't?"

---

## What YC is actually evaluating

YC is checking two things: first, that you have honest awareness of the competitive landscape (no "there are no competitors" answers); second, that you have a genuine insight — something you understand about the problem, the buyer, or the market that your competitors have missed or ignored. The insight is the important part. A list of competitors without an insight reads as market research, not strategy.

---

## What strong answers contain

- Named competitors, including indirect substitutes (what customers use today, even if it's a spreadsheet or a manual process)
- A specific insight stated in one or two sentences: "They serve [buyer A] because [reason]. We've found that [buyer B] has the same pain with higher urgency and no solution — and [buyer A] players can't easily serve them because [structural reason]."
- An explanation of why the insight is durable — why incumbents can't just copy the approach
- The wedge customer segment: who competitors are ignoring that you're targeting first
- Evidence that the insight came from research or experience, not just reasoning

---

## What weak answers look like

- "There are no direct competitors" — almost never true, and signals poor research
- Lists Salesforce, Microsoft, or Google without explaining why the applicant can compete with them
- "We're different because we use AI" — every company says this; it's not an insight
- "Our competitive advantage is our team" — team is not a market insight
- "We will outcompete because we're faster and more agile" — a plan, not an insight
- Naming competitors correctly but failing to state any specific insight about what they're missing

---

## Probing questions to ask the founder

1. "What do potential customers use today to solve this problem? Even if it's a spreadsheet or a manual process, name it."
2. "Have you signed up for and used the top 3 competitors? What did you find that you can't find anywhere publicly?"
3. "What do competitors get wrong that you get right? Give me one sentence."
4. "Why can't the largest player in this space just add a feature that competes with you?"
5. "Which competitor do you respect most and why? What would it take to beat them specifically?"

---

## Evidence to request from the founder's local files

- Competitive analysis notes or documents
- Screenshots or accounts in competitor products (shows they've actually used them)
- Customer feedback that explicitly mentions switching from a competitor or comparing to one
- Any recorded user interviews where the competitor landscape came up

---

## Coaching note on the insight question

The insight question is where most founders leave the most points on the table. They name competitors correctly but then answer "what do you understand that they don't" with a feature list ("we have better UX, faster onboarding, lower price"). That's not an insight — that's a positioning statement. The real answer is structural: "They built for [buyer X] because that's the easiest to reach, but [buyer Y] has 3x the pain, spends 3x as much on the problem, and is systematically underserved because [specific reason competitors can't serve them]." Push founders to get to that level.
